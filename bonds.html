How to read the Bond & Yield Monitor
The bond market is the absolute thermometer of the financial system. Yields anticipate rate decisions, reflect growth expectations, and signal risk appetite before equity markets react.

Yield Curve shows the relationship between short-term and long-term rates. A normal curve (long rates above short rates) signals healthy growth expectations. An inverted curve (2Y above 10Y) has preceded every US recession since the 1970s. The 2Y-10Y spread is the most watched inversion signal.

Real Yields (TIPS) measure the return after inflation. They have a strong inverse correlation with Gold — when real yields fall, gold tends to rise as the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets drops.

Credit Spreads measure the premium investors demand for corporate bonds over Treasuries. IG (Investment Grade) widening signals caution; HY (High Yield) spiking signals financial stress. Tight spreads = risk-on, wide spreads = risk-off.

Fed Funds Rate is the overnight lending rate set by the Fed. The gap between the effective rate and the target range shows market stress. When effective drifts toward the upper bound, funding pressure is building.

Key insight: when yields fall, the dollar tends to weaken and risk assets rise. When yields rise but the dollar falls simultaneously, it signals a credibility shock — markets losing confidence in the issuer rather than repricing growth.
