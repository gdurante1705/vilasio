How to read the Liquidity Monitor
Net Liquidity is the single most important macro variable for risk assets. It measures how much money is actually available in the financial system. The formula is simple: Net Liquidity = WALCL − TGA − Reverse Repo. Each component plays a distinct role in determining how much capital is circulating.

WALCL (Fed Total Assets) is the source of liquidity — when the Fed buys bonds (QE), WALCL increases and liquidity rises. During QT, WALCL shrinks.

TGA (Treasury General Account) is the government's checking account at the Fed. When TGA is high, cash is parked there instead of circulating — liquidity decreases. When TGA drains (government spending), liquidity is released into the system.

Reverse Repo (ON RRP) is where institutions park excess cash overnight with the Fed. High RRP means cash sitting on the sidelines. As RRP declines, that cash re-enters markets.

Key insight: S&P 500 and Net Liquidity are highly correlated over medium-term horizons. Divergences between the two often resolve with price catching up to liquidity. When Net Liquidity expands while S&P 500 lags — it tends to be a buying opportunity. When S&P 500 leads while liquidity contracts — caution is warranted.
